How it works

The leasing team enters a unit. Kestrel returns a dossier.

A pricing recommendation that cannot be defended is worth nothing. Kestrel inverts the usual flow: every unit input produces a one-page dossier with the evidence, the scarcity read, the lease term and incentive guidance, and the narrative behind the recommended weekly rent. The leasing team reviews. The asset manager signs.

  1. 01

    Enter the unit

    The operator enters as much as influences price: address, type, bedrooms, bathrooms, parking, view, floor, recent works, target start date. Kestrel accepts what is known and continues without what is not.

  2. 02

    Assemble the catchment

    Kestrel pulls the closest structural matches inside a strict local catchment, widening the radius only if the cohort is too small to defend. Every comp is a real listing with a real address.

  3. 03

    Read the scarcity signal

    Days-on-market, active listings, lease-up velocity, vacancy, supply pipeline, migrant arrivals, employment growth, price-cut frequency. Eight signals, weighted and z-scored, land on a band from Glut to Acute.

  4. 04

    Produce the dossier

    A one-page document: recommended weekly rent with confidence, the comp table, the scarcity panel, the lease term and incentive matrix, and a short narrative where every sentence cites a data point.

  5. 05

    Review, sign, log

    The leasing team can accept, override, or annotate. The asset manager signs. Every dossier is archived against its run-id with the comps, the model, the overrides, the reviewer, and the timestamp.

The dossier

One page. Every recommendation. Every time.

The dossier is the product. Eight components, one page, signed by a named human. Built to be read by a leasing executive, an asset manager, an investment committee, or a regulator without changing a word.

01 Header

Recommended weekly rent, confidence, lease term, incentive.

The number first, then the confidence band, the recommended lease term, and the structured incentive guidance. Everything the leasing executive needs to quote a prospect at the front desk in five seconds.

02 Comp table

Five to eight closest structural matches with addresses.

Real listings, real addresses, real asking rents. Sorted by structural distance. Every comp is a row a human can recognise, walk past, and verify. No abstracted suburb medians standing in for evidence.

03 Scarcity panel

A quadrant plot and a band: Glut, Soft, Balanced, Tight, Acute.

Eight weighted signals, z-scored against the national distribution. A unit in an Acute suburb prices differently from the same unit in a Glut suburb, and the dossier shows the operator exactly why.

04 Charts

Rent distribution, days-on-market curve, 12-month trend, yield.

Four small multiples on one row. Enough to see where this unit sits in the market and how the market has moved. Not enough to drown the page.

05 Signal tiles

Transport, schools, amenity, supply, demand, planning.

A grid of small tiles, each summarising a structured signal that influenced the recommendation. Hover for the source and the value. Every tile is clickable through to the underlying data.

06 Narrative

Around 200 words. Every sentence cites a data point.

Not generic prose. The narrative reads the comp table, the scarcity panel, the charts, and the signal tiles, and explains in plain English why this weekly rent is the right one and what would move it.

07 Lease term and incentive

Five rows with a recommended row highlighted.

Six, nine, twelve, eighteen, twenty-four months. For each, the suggested weekly rent, the suggested incentive (weeks free, fitout, white goods), and the expected net yield. The leasing team has the negotiation matrix on the page.

08 Sensitivity

Three scenarios: speed, balanced, max yield.

The same unit priced for the fastest lease-up, the balanced view, and the highest defensible yield. The operator chooses which scenario the property runs on, and the dossier shows the trade.

The dossier is exportable as a single PDF. Every dossier is archived against its run-id. Six months later, an asset manager can pull the exact dossier that drove a specific decision and read it as it was signed.

Why it matters

Five things we will not compromise on.

01

Single-tenant, no pooled data

The dominant North American pricing tools aggregate competitive data across all their clients. That is the design choice now under DOJ consent decree in the US, state-level bans accelerating, and increasing regulatory attention in Australia. Kestrel does not pool. Each operator's catchment data drives only their own dossiers.

02

Provenance, not a number

A pricing recommendation that arrives without evidence is a guess in a suit. Every Kestrel recommendation comes with a one-page dossier the leasing team can read, defend, and sign. That is the product. The number is a by-product of the evidence.

03

Scarcity-aware

The same unit in the same suburb prices differently when stock is acute and when stock is in glut. Kestrel reads eight structured scarcity signals and lands a band on every dossier. Operators see the market they are pricing into, not just the comp table.

04

Assistant, not autopilot

Kestrel proposes. The leasing executive applies context. The asset manager reviews and signs. Every override is captured with structured reasons. The asset owner can trace any rent decision back to the dossier, the comps, the model, the override, and the named person who signed it.

05

Australian-native

Built in Australia, against Australian listing data, with Australian catchment radii, against the Australian PMS landscape, and with the Australian regulatory tailwind in mind. We speak the language of postcodes, suburbs, weekly rent, and the local agent ecosystem.
